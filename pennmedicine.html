Context

During my Master's at the University of Pennsylvania, my team was approached by Penn Medicine to help address a rising safety concern flagged by their internal risk team—tourniquets were being forgotten on patient limbs during surgery, causing patient harm and liability risks for the hospital.

In collaboration with Penn Medicine and Wharton's incubator program, I spent over a year working alongside clinicians, hospital leadership, and venture advisors to produce a solution.

As Product Lead, I led a team of industrial designers and mechanical engineers to develop a functional prototype that would guarantee patient safety—without adding unnecessary steps or pressure to clinical workflows.

Problem

Research

We started our project by pulling on scrubs and stepping into the OR environment. Over the course of the engagement, we conducted in-depth primary research with 37 clinicians—including medical students, attending surgeons, nurses, and anesthesiologists. This included a mix of interviews, full-day shadowing, and direct observation of surgical procedures.

By immersing ourselves in their everyday routines, we were able to observe user behaviours and pinpoint exactly when and why tourniquet errors may occur. From those insights, we identified three key opportunity areas for intervention.

Ideation

We kicked off with a round of divergent brainstorming, generating 50+ ideas to spark discussion and challenge assumptions. From there, we built quick prototypes using off-the-shelf components and left them with clinicians to test over the course of a week—directly in their hospital environment.

This rapid feedback loop allowed us to eliminate any concepts that would create barriers to user adoption—whether by adding time, increasing cognitive load, or disrupting workflows. It also led to a key shift in direction: rather than putting the onus on clinicians to prevent accidents, how might we offer them a safeguard for when human error inevitably occurs?

Prototyping

With the goal of creating an auto-release tourniquet device, we began exploring how the concept could work in practice—focusing on core user interactions and technical engineering.

Through nine rounds of iteration, we moved from early form factor studies to a fully functional prototype with a reliable mechanical release and embedded electronic components.

Each version was tested with clinicians at Penn Medicine, enabling us to co-design a solution that meets their needs and integrates into existing workflows.

Product

We created a device that transforms how tourniquets are applied. After 10 minutes, the device automatically releases the tourniquet from patient limbs, adding a critical layer of safety without requiring any additional steps or attention.

Every aspect of the product was designed for easy adoption—intuitive for clinicians, comfortable for patients, and cost-efficient to produce—with a projected 81% profit margin and $400K in annual savings per hospital. It's a solution that works with the system, not against it.

With support from Wharton's Venture Lab incubator, we grew the project into a venture and secured a provisional utility patent for the invention. We've since handed off the final prototype to Penn Medicine, who are now preparing it for clinical trials.
